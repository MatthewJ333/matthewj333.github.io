World Happiness Scores

CONCEPT:

This project explores a way to shed light on a somewhat complex dataset, based on the factors to why people are either happy or unhappy with where and how they live. The idea for this project was based on the assumption that living in close proximity to oceans and having a robust economy could prove to facilitate a better quality of life, and further oneâ€™s life expectancy.

DATA:

The dataset was found via Google (Career Certificates) on Tableau Public. It covered three years (2015-2017) of global records that included happiness ratings spanning from life expectancy, trust of the government, the economy, generosity, family and freedom.

Given the range of criteria to analyze, I initially focused on each year, but found that the data did not fluctuate drastically throughout each time period, so I expanded the range to cover the entire duration of the records to more closely reflect the true depth of each data variable. After further analysis, it was evident that there were many correlations for happiness between first world countries that had a stable economy, strong family relations, a firm sense of freedom, which in turn offered good health for longer life expectancy, and more generosity. Some of which also displayed a hearty sense of trust in their governments, based on perceived corruption.

PROCESS:

After analysis of the data, I felt it best to display the findings and generate further insights out of it by using an interactive scatter plot visualization with trend lines, which I found to be the best representation of the data without bias.
